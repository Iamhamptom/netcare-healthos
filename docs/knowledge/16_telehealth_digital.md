## SECTION A: TELEMEDICINE BILLING CODES & TARIFFS

### A1. Telehealth Tariff Codes

South Africa does not have a single unified tariff schedule since the NHRPL was discontinued in 2010. Each medical scheme publishes its own rates. However, the following codes are widely recognised across schemes:

| Code | Description | Type | Notes |
|------|-------------|------|-------|
| **0190** | Consultation — average duration/complexity (~15 min) | In-person / Video | Standard GP consultation |
| **0191** | Consultation — above-average duration/complexity (~30 min) | In-person / Video | Extended consultation |
| **0192** | Consultation — long duration/high complexity (~45 min) | In-person / Video | Complex consultation |
| **0130** | Telephonic conversation and/or prescription | Telephonic only | Dedicated telephonic code |
| **0128** | Brief telephonic consultation | Telephonic only | Short phone interaction |
| **0197** | Audio/video virtual consultation | Virtual (telehealth) | Scheme-specific; not universally adopted |

**Key billing rules:**
- Codes 0190-0192 are primarily face-to-face codes; some schemes accept them for video consultations at the same rate
- Code 0130 is the established telephonic consultation code — lower rate than in-person
- Non-face-to-face codes (0128, 0130) CANNOT be billed together with face-to-face codes (0190-0192) on the same day for the same patient
- Some schemes have introduced scheme-specific virtual consultation codes (e.g., Discovery's Connected Care codes)
- Only ONE consultation code may be billed per patient per day

### A2. Rate Comparison: Telehealth vs In-Person

| Service | Typical In-Person Rate | Typical Telehealth Rate | Difference |
|---------|----------------------|------------------------|------------|
| GP consultation (0190) | R480 — R650 | R250 — R500 | ~30-40% lower |
| Extended consult (0191) | R650 — R900 | R400 — R700 | ~25-35% lower |
| Complex consult (0192) | R900 — R1,200 | R600 — R950 | ~20-30% lower |
| Telephonic (0130) | N/A | R250 — R380 | Lowest rate |
| Online platform (Kena/Udok) | N/A | R235 — R299 | Fixed-price platforms |

**Important:** Each scheme sets its own tariff. Discovery, GEMS, Bonitas, and Momentum all have different rate structures. Virtual consultations generally reimburse at 60-80% of in-person rates when billed under telephonic codes.

### A3. After-Hours Modifiers for Telehealth

After-hours modifiers CAN apply to telehealth consultations if the scheme permits:

| Modifier Code | Description | Add-On |
|--------------|-------------|--------|
| **0148** | Elective after-hours (non-emergency, patient request) | +50% of base consultation fee |
| **0149** | Emergency after-hours (21:00-06:00 daily) | +25% of base consultation fee |

- Modifier 0148 applies to codes 0190-0193, 0173-0175, 0161-0164, 0166-0169, 0151-0153
- Modifier 0149 applies to the same codes plus item 0113
- Whether these modifiers apply to telehealth varies by scheme — confirm with each scheme's provider relations unit
- Many schemes do NOT recognise after-hours modifiers for telephonic consultations (code 0130)
- Rationale: telehealth reduces overhead, so schemes may argue against premium pricing

## SECTION C: TELEHEALTH REFERRALS & PRESCRIPTIONS

### C1. Specialist Referrals via Telehealth

**Can telehealth generate referrals? YES — with conditions:**

- A GP may issue a referral to a specialist based on a telehealth consultation
- The referral letter/authorisation must document the clinical rationale
- The specialist consultation itself can also be conducted via telehealth
- Specialist telemedicine is the BULK of SA telemedicine practice (HPCSA noted) — particularly for rural access
- Some schemes require the initial GP visit to be in-person before specialist referral
- Pre-authorisation requirements for specialist visits still apply regardless of consultation mode
- The referral must specify whether the specialist consultation will be virtual or in-person

### C2. Prescriptions via Telehealth

**Prescribing rules by schedule:**

| Schedule | Telehealth Prescribing | Restrictions |
|----------|----------------------|--------------|
| **S0** | Allowed | Over-the-counter, no prescription needed |
| **S1-S2** | Allowed | Standard prescription requirements apply |
| **S3** | Allowed | Prescription required; repeats permitted |
| **S4** | Allowed with caution | Prescription required; repeats up to 6 months |
| **S5** | Restricted | Potential for abuse/dependence; strict management required; repeats beyond 6 months permitted but tightly controlled |
| **S6** | Highly restricted / Generally NOT recommended | 30-day supply max; NO repeat prescriptions; new written prescription each time; high abuse potential |
| **S7-S8** | NOT permitted via telehealth | Controlled substances; special permit required |

**HPCSA prescription rules for telehealth:**
- Prescription based SOLELY on an online questionnaire is NOT permitted — does not meet standard of care
- A routine medical assessment with relevant patient clinical history MUST precede prescribing
- Existing practitioner-patient relationship is preferred but not compulsory (HPCSA Booklet 10, Dec 2021)
- Electronic prescriptions require an advanced electronic signature per Section 13 of the ECT Act 2002
- The prescribing practitioner retains full responsibility for treatment decisions
- E-prescriptions sent directly from prescriber to pharmacy are preferred for authentication

### C3. E-Prescribing Workflow

**Legal framework:** Medicines and Related Substances Act (Act 101 of 1965) + Electronic Communications and Transactions Act (Act 25 of 2002)

**Workflow:**
1. Practitioner conducts telehealth consultation with clinical assessment
2. Prescription generated with advanced electronic signature (ECT Act Section 13)
3. Prescription transmitted directly to pharmacy (preferred) or to patient
4. Pharmacist authenticates prescription — direct transmission from prescriber is most reliable
5. Pharmacist dispenses medication per Pharmacy Act requirements
6. Record of prescription kept by both practitioner and pharmacist

**Requirements for valid e-prescription:**
- Advanced electronic signature (from accredited service provider)
- Patient identification details
- Practitioner details and practice number
- Date of consultation and prescription
- Diagnosis (ICD-10 code)
- Medication name, dosage, frequency, quantity
- Number of repeats authorised (if applicable)
- "Substitution not permitted" notation if applicable

**SAPC (SA Pharmacy Council) position:**
- Encourages electronic transmission directly from prescriber to pharmacy
- Pharmacist bears responsibility for authenticating prescriptions received by other means (fax, WhatsApp, email)
- Faxed/verbal prescriptions must have permanent copy made for records

## SECTION D: TELEHEALTH DOCUMENTATION REQUIREMENTS

### D1. Clinical Documentation for Telehealth Claims

Every telehealth consultation must document:

| Element | Required Content |
|---------|-----------------|
| **Patient identity** | Full name, ID number, medical scheme details |
| **Practitioner identity** | Name, practice number, HPCSA registration |
| **Date and time** | Date, start time, duration of consultation |
| **Consultation mode** | Telephonic, video, text-based, or hybrid |
| **Platform used** | Name of technology platform (DrConnect, WhatsUpDoctor, etc.) |
| **Informed consent** | Documented consent for telehealth interaction |
| **Clinical history** | Presenting complaint, relevant medical history |
| **Assessment** | Clinical findings (what could be assessed remotely) |
| **Limitations noted** | What could NOT be assessed remotely |
| **Diagnosis** | ICD-10 code(s) |
| **Treatment plan** | Medications prescribed, investigations ordered, referrals made |
| **Follow-up** | Plan for follow-up (virtual or in-person) |
| **Safety netting** | When to seek in-person/emergency care |

### D2. Informed Consent for Telehealth

HPCSA Booklet 10 (December 2021) requires informed consent to include:

- Types of transmissions consented to (x-rays, test results, clinical reports, advice, prescriptions, appointments)
- Security measures taken to protect patient information
- Possible risks to breaches of confidentiality
- Limitations of telehealth (inability to perform physical examination)
- Right to withdraw consent at any time
- Alternative to telehealth (in-person consultation)

**Consent may be verbal or written** — but written/electronic documentation is strongly recommended for medico-legal protection.

### D3. Record Retention

- Clinical records must be kept for the patient's lifetime + 10 years (HPCSA)
- Billing records must be kept separate from clinical records
- Electronic records must be backed up and encrypted
- Records must be accessible for medical scheme audits and clinical reviews

## SECTION E: DIGITAL HEALTH SERVICES

### E1. Remote Patient Monitoring (RPM)

**SA billing status:** No dedicated RPM tariff codes exist in South Africa's medical billing framework as of 2026.

| Aspect | Current Status |
|--------|---------------|
| Billing code | None — no SA-specific CPT/tariff code for RPM |
| Reimbursement | Not directly reimbursable as a standalone service |
| Workarounds | Bill as follow-up consultation (0190) when reviewing RPM data |
| Chronic programmes | Discovery/GEMS chronic disease management may cover RPM devices |
| Device costs | Generally patient-funded (blood glucose monitors, BP monitors, etc.) |
| Data transmission | Covered under general telehealth consultation when practitioner reviews data |
| Future outlook | Expected to evolve as NHI and digital health policy develops |

**How schemes handle RPM currently:**
- Discovery: Connected Care includes some remote monitoring for chronic patients; devices may be covered under chronic benefit
- GEMS: No specific RPM benefit; chronic management consultations cover data review
- Bonitas: WhatsUpDoctor allows sharing of monitoring results; consultation covers review
- Momentum: Hello Doctor can review patient-submitted data; no RPM-specific benefit

### E2. Chronic Disease Monitoring via Apps

**Billable? Indirectly — through consultation codes when a practitioner reviews data.**

- App-based monitoring (glucose logs, BP readings, symptom diaries) is NOT independently billable
- When a practitioner conducts a consultation (telehealth or in-person) to review app data, the consultation is billable
- Discovery's Connected Care and Vitality programme integrate with health apps (Apple Health, Fitbit, Garmin)
- Chronic patients on CDL conditions can have telehealth follow-ups billed to chronic benefit
- AI-driven alerts from monitoring apps do not generate a billable event — only practitioner review does

### E3. AI-Assisted Triage

**Discovery's DrConnect model:**
- "Ask a Doctor" feature provides AI-assisted initial triage
- Free to all Discovery members — no billing event
- Bot-initiated symptom assessment → routed to appropriate care level
- NOT a replacement for clinical consultation — triage only
- No medical scheme currently reimburses AI-only triage as a billable event

**General AI triage models in SA:**
- AI chatbots for symptom checking: free value-add, not billable
- AI-assisted clinical decision support during consultation: billable as part of the consultation
- AI triage that results in a practitioner consultation: the consultation is billable, not the AI step
- Regulatory gap: HPCSA has not issued specific guidelines on AI-assisted diagnosis billing

### E4. Digital Mental Health

**Telepsychology and telepsychiatry rules:**

| Aspect | Rule |
|--------|------|
| HPCSA recognition | Telepsychology, telepsychiatry, telerehabilitation explicitly included in Booklet 10 |
| Session limits | Scheme-dependent — typically 12-15 sessions per year per beneficiary |
| Billing codes | Standard psychology/psychiatry consultation codes apply |
| Platform | Any HPCSA-compliant secure platform |
| Practitioner | Must be registered psychologist/psychiatrist with HPCSA |
| In-person requirement | Initial assessment in-person is recommended but not mandatory (post-2021 revision) |
| PMB coverage | Mental health conditions on DTP list are PMBs — schemes MUST cover regardless of mode |
| Discovery | Mental Health Care Programme covers telepsychology; unlimited PMB sessions for severe conditions |
| GEMS | Psychology benefit varies by option; telehealth delivery accepted |
| Bonitas | Limited psychology sessions per option; virtual delivery permitted |
| Momentum | Psychology benefit included; virtual sessions count toward annual limit |

**Key considerations:**
- Online-only counselling practitioners must still be HPCSA-registered
- HPCSA discourages purely social media-based therapy (Facebook, Instagram)
- Secure video platforms are preferred over WhatsApp for therapy sessions
- Group therapy via video: permitted but consent from all participants required
- Crisis intervention: must have in-person fallback plan documented

## SECTION F: WHATSAPP IN HEALTHCARE

### F1. HPCSA Position on WhatsApp

**Status: Cautiously permitted with significant caveats**

| Aspect | HPCSA Position |
|--------|---------------|
| Clinical communication | "Any suitable ICT platform" may be used (Booklet 10) |
| Social media caution | HPCSA discourages social media for patient communication — risk of ethical dilemmas |
| Professional boundaries | Must maintain "strictly professional relationships" with patients |
| Data security | WhatsApp data crosses international borders — potential POPIA conflict |
| End-to-end encryption | WhatsApp provides E2E encryption but SA data may be stored in foreign jurisdictions |
| Clinical use | Acceptable for clinical communication IF proper safeguards in place |
| Recommendation | Practices should have a comprehensive electronic/social media communications policy |

### F2. WhatsApp Appointment Reminders

**Permitted — with POPIA consent:**

- **Consent required:** Explicit opt-in consent before sending appointment reminders
- **Consent type:** Written or electronic consent documenting permission for WhatsApp communication
- **Content restrictions:** Appointment date, time, location only — no clinical details in reminders
- **Opt-out:** Patient must be able to opt out at any time (reply "STOP")
- **POPIA Section 69:** Direct marketing via electronic communication requires prior consent
- **Penalty:** Non-compliance with POPIA — fines up to R10 million
- **Best practice:** Use WhatsApp Business API (not personal WhatsApp) for audit trail and compliance

### F3. Lab Results via WhatsApp

**Legally problematic — high risk:**

| Risk | Detail |
|------|--------|
| Data security | Lab results are "special personal information" under POPIA Section 26 |
| International servers | WhatsApp data transits through servers outside SA — POPIA cross-border transfer rules apply |
| Patient identification | Risk of sending to wrong WhatsApp number — no identity verification |
| Legal liability | Practitioner liable for data breach if results reach wrong person |
| HPCSA ethics | Sharing patient information only justifiable when patient has consented AND information goes to treating team |
| Best practice | Use secure patient portal or encrypted platform instead of WhatsApp |
| If WhatsApp used | Verify patient identity, obtain explicit written consent, do NOT include identifying patient details in the same message as results |

### F4. WhatsApp Business API Integration Patterns

**For medical practices in South Africa:**

| Pattern | Implementation |
|---------|---------------|
| Appointment reminders | Automated 24-hour pre-visit reminders; quick-reply confirm/reschedule/cancel |
| Appointment booking | Chatbot-assisted booking with calendar integration |
| Follow-up care | Post-visit care tips, recovery guidelines, medication reminders |
| Prescription refills | Patient requests refill → routed to practitioner for approval |
| Feedback collection | Post-consultation satisfaction surveys |
| Recall management | Automated recall reminders for annual check-ups, screenings |
| Payment links | Send payment request links for co-payments/shortfalls |
| Waiting room | Virtual check-in and queue management |

**Integration requirements:**
- WhatsApp Business API (not regular WhatsApp) — requires Meta Business verification
- POPIA-compliant data processing agreement
- Message templates must be pre-approved by WhatsApp/Meta
- Must use official Business Solution Provider (BSP) — e.g., Twilio, MessageBird, Clickatell (SA-based)
- Patient opt-in/opt-out management
- Message logging and audit trail
- No clinical data in automated messages

### F5. Claim Status Notifications via WhatsApp

**Current scheme adoption:**

| Scheme | WhatsApp Status |
|--------|----------------|
| Discovery | "Ask Discovery" on WhatsApp — claims, benefits, rewards updates; E2E encrypted |
| GEMS | No WhatsApp service — Member App for claims tracking |
| Bonitas | WhatsUpDoctor for clinical; no claims status via WhatsApp |
| Momentum | Hello Doctor for clinical; claims via Momentum app |
| Medihelp | SMS-based claim notifications |
| Fedhealth | App-based claim tracking |

- Discovery is the most advanced — personalised claims and benefits updates via WhatsApp

### G3. Payment Plans for Medical Shortfalls

**Legal framework:**

- **National Credit Act (NCA) — Act 34 of 2005:** Payment plans offered by medical practices MAY fall under the NCA if they constitute "credit agreements"
- **When NCA applies:** If the practice charges interest or fees on the outstanding amount, or if the payment period exceeds the original service date by more than a specific period
- **When NCA does NOT apply:** If the practice simply agrees to accept payment in instalments without interest or additional charges (informal arrangement)
- **Incidental credit:** If a doctor simply allows a patient to pay later without interest, this is generally exempt from NCA registration requirements
- **Formal credit:** If the practice offers a structured payment plan with terms, conditions, and interest, it may need to register as a credit provider with the NCR

**Best practices for payment plans:**
- Document the agreement in writing
- Specify the total amount, instalment amounts, and dates
- State clearly whether interest applies (avoid interest to stay outside NCA)
- Get patient signature
- Keep payment plan records separate from clinical records

### G4. Medical Debt Collection Rules

**Governing legislation:**
- Prescription Act 68 of 1969
- Debt Collectors Act 114 of 1998
- National Credit Act 34 of 2005 (where applicable)
- Consumer Protection Act 68 of 2008

**Key rules:**

| Rule | Detail |
|------|--------|
| Prescription period | Medical debts prescribe (expire) after **3 years** from date due |
| Interruption | Prescription interrupted by: (a) written acknowledgment of debt, (b) partial payment, (c) service of legal process |
| Default notice | Creditor must give 20 business days written notice of default before legal action |
| Debtor's options | Within 10 days of notice: pay arrears, make arrangement, or refer to debt counsellor |
| Debt collectors | Must be registered with the Council for Debt Collectors |
| Prohibited conduct | Harassment, threats, contacting employer, excessive contact |
| Business hours | Debt collectors may only contact debtors during reasonable hours |
| Itemised account | Patient entitled to request itemised statement of all charges |
| Medical aid claims | Practice should first exhaust medical aid claim before pursuing patient for shortfall |

### G5. Gap Cover Claim Automation

**How gap cover works in SA healthcare:**

Gap cover is a short-term insurance product that covers the "gap" between what medical aid pays and what the provider charges. It is NOT part of the medical scheme — it is a separate insurance policy underwritten by insurers like Guardrisk.

**Key providers:**
- MedGap (underwritten by Guardrisk Insurance)
- Momentum Gap Cover
- Old Mutual Gap Cover
- Kaelo Gap
- Medway
- Admed (Guardrisk subsidiary)

**MedGap/Guardrisk automation:**
1. Member consents to claims data sharing between medical scheme and Guardrisk
2. When medical aid pays less than provider charges, the shortfall is identified
3. If data-sharing consent exists: claim is **automatically processed and paid** to member
4. If no consent: member must submit claim manually within 4 months of treatment date
5. Missing documents: member has 28 days to provide after request, or claim is closed
6. Payment: directly to member (not to provider)

**HealthOps integration opportunity:**
- Detect shortfall at point of billing
- Prompt patient to submit gap cover claim
- Generate gap cover claim form with pre-populated data
- Track gap cover payment status
- Reconcile gap cover payments against outstanding patient balance

## SECTION H: COVID-19 LEGACY & PERMANENT CHANGES

### H1. COVID-Era Telehealth Expansions That Became Permanent

| Change | Pre-COVID | During COVID | Post-COVID (Permanent) |
|--------|-----------|-------------|----------------------|
| HPCSA terminology | "Telemedicine" | "Telehealth" | **"Telehealth"** — broader definition retained |
| Scope | Telemedicine only | Added telepsychology, telepsychiatry, telerehabilitation | **All sub-disciplines retained** |
| Prior relationship | Required (strongly recommended) | Waived during pandemic | **Not compulsory** (Booklet 10, Dec 2021) |
| First consultation | In-person preferred | Virtual allowed | **Virtual first consultation permitted** |
| Platform flexibility | Limited guidance | Any suitable ICT platform | **Any suitable ICT platform** confirmed |
| Prescribing | Restricted | Expanded | **Expanded rules retained** (with clinical assessment requirement) |
| Mental health | Limited telehealth | Full telepsychology permitted | **Telepsychology permanently permitted** |
| Scheme coverage | Limited/experimental | Emergency coverage expanded | **Most schemes now cover telehealth as standard benefit** |

### H2. HPCSA Updated Telehealth Guidelines (December 2021)

**Booklet 10 — General Ethical Guidelines for Good Practice in Telehealth (Revised)**

Key changes from the 2014 version:

1. **Name change:** "Telemedicine" → "Telehealth" — broader scope
2. **Sub-disciplines:** Explicitly includes telepsychology, telepsychiatry, telerehabilitation
3. **Prior relationship:** Desirable but NOT compulsory
4. **Platform:** "Any suitable ICT platform" — not limited to specific technology
5. **Standard of care:** Same standard as in-person — practitioner fully responsible
6. **Informed consent:** Must cover types of transmissions, security measures, risks
7. **Record keeping:** Detailed records required including mode of consultation
8. **Confidentiality:** All privacy obligations continue to apply
9. **Cross-border:** SA practitioners may consult SA patients regardless of location within SA
10. **Ongoing review:** HPCSA committed to continuing updates as telehealth evolves

## QUICK REFERENCE: TELEHEALTH BILLING DECISION TREE

```
Is this a telehealth consultation?
├── YES → What mode?
│   ├── Video call → Bill 0190/0191/0192 (if scheme accepts for video)
│   │   └── Some schemes: use scheme-specific virtual code
│   ├── Phone call → Bill 0130 (telephonic)
│   └── Text/WhatsApp → Bill 0130 or scheme-specific code
│       └── Bonitas BonCap: via WhatsUpDoctor
├── Is it after hours?
│   ├── YES, elective (patient requested) → Add modifier 0148 (+50%)
│   ├── YES, emergency (21:00-06:00) → Add modifier 0149 (+25%)
│   └── NO → Standard rate
├── Is this a follow-up for chronic condition?
│   ├── YES → Bill to chronic benefit (CDL conditions)
│   └── NO → Bill to day-to-day benefit
├── Does patient need prescription?
│   ├── S0-S4 → Prescribe via e-prescription
│   ├── S5 → Prescribe with caution, document clinical rationale
│   ├── S6 → Generally avoid telehealth prescribing; in-person preferred
│   └── S7-S8 → NOT permitted via telehealth
├── Does patient need specialist referral?
│   ├── YES → Issue referral; check scheme pre-auth requirements
│   └── Specialist can also consult via telehealth
└── Documentation complete?
    ├── Consent recorded → YES
    ├── Clinical notes complete → YES
    ├── ICD-10 code assigned → YES
    ├── Mode of consultation documented → YES
    └── SUBMIT CLAIM
```
