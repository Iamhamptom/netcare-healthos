## 1. Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus — SEMDSA Guidelines

**Source**: SEMDSA (Society for Endocrinology, Metabolism and Diabetes of SA) Guidelines for the Management of Type 2 Diabetes, 4th Edition (2017), published in JEMDSA 2017;22(1)(Supplement 1):S1-S196. Updated with 2024 position statements.

### 1.1 Diagnostic Criteria

| Test | Diabetes | Impaired (Pre-diabetes) | Normal |
|------|----------|------------------------|--------|
| Fasting Plasma Glucose (FPG) | >= 7.0 mmol/L | 6.1–6.9 mmol/L (IFG) | < 6.1 mmol/L |
| 2-hour OGTT (75g glucose) | >= 11.1 mmol/L | 7.8–11.0 mmol/L (IGT) | < 7.8 mmol/L |
| Random/Casual Glucose (symptomatic) | >= 11.1 mmol/L | — | — |
| HbA1c | >= 6.5% (48 mmol/mol) | 5.7–6.4% | < 5.7% |

**Confirmatory testing**: In asymptomatic patients, diagnosis requires TWO abnormal results (same or different tests) on separate occasions. In symptomatic patients (polyuria, polydipsia, weight loss), a single abnormal result suffices.

### 1.2 HbA1c Targets

| Patient Profile | HbA1c Target |
|----------------|-------------|
| General target (most patients) | < 7.0% (53 mmol/mol) |
| Younger, newly diagnosed, no CVD, low hypoglycaemia risk | < 6.5% (48 mmol/mol) |
| Elderly, long-standing diabetes, CVD, hypoglycaemia risk | < 8.0% (64 mmol/mol) |
| Frail elderly / limited life expectancy | < 8.5% (69 mmol/mol) |

### 1.3 Step Therapy Algorithm

**STEP 1 — Lifestyle + Metformin (First-Line)**
- Lifestyle modification at diagnosis (diet, exercise, weight management)
- Start metformin immediately unless contraindicated
- Initiate at 500 mg daily, titrate over 1–2 months to maximum 2,550 mg/day (850 mg TDS or 1,000 mg BD)
- Contraindications: eGFR < 30 mL/min, acute illness, severe hepatic disease
- Reduce dose if eGFR 30–45 mL/min
- HbA1c reduction: 1.0–2.0%
- **Review at 3 months**: If HbA1c not at target, proceed to Step 2

**STEP 2 — Add Sulphonylurea (SU) — Dual Therapy**
- Add gliclazide (preferred SU) 80–320 mg/day or glimepiride 1–4 mg/day
- HbA1c reduction: additional 1.0–2.0%
- Warn patient about hypoglycaemia risk
- Avoid glibenclamide in elderly (long-acting, higher hypo risk)
- **Review at 3 months**: If HbA1c not at target, proceed to Step 3

**STEP 3 — Add Third Agent — Triple Therapy**
Options (add to metformin + SU):
- **DPP-4 inhibitor** (sitagliptin, vildagliptin, saxagliptin): HbA1c reduction 0.5–0.8%. Weight neutral, low hypo risk
- **SGLT2 inhibitor** (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin): HbA1c reduction 0.5–1.0%. Weight loss, BP reduction, cardio-renal benefits. Preferred if CVD or CKD present
- **Thiazolidinedione** (pioglitazone): HbA1c reduction 0.5–1.4%. Risk of weight gain, oedema, fractures
- **GLP-1 receptor agonist** (liraglutide, semaglutide): HbA1c reduction 1.0–1.5%. Weight loss, CV benefit. Injectable
- **Review at 3 months**: If HbA1c not at target, proceed to Step 4

**STEP 4 — Insulin Initiation**
- Start basal insulin (NPH at bedtime or long-acting analogue — glargine/detemir)
- Starting dose: 10 units or 0.1–0.2 U/kg at bedtime
- Titrate by 2 units every 3 days until fasting glucose 4.0–7.0 mmol/L
- Continue metformin; consider stopping SU if hypoglycaemia occurs
- If HbA1c remains above target on basal insulin > 0.5 U/kg: add prandial insulin (basal-bolus) or premixed insulin

**STEP 5 — Intensified Insulin**
- Basal-bolus regimen (basal + rapid-acting insulin before meals)
- Or premixed insulin BD (e.g., Humalog Mix 25, NovoMix 30)
- Refer to endocrinologist/diabetologist if HbA1c > 9% despite optimised triple therapy + insulin

### 1.4 Monitoring Schedule

| Test | Frequency |
|------|-----------|
| HbA1c | Every 3 months until at target, then every 6 months |
| Self-monitoring blood glucose (SMBG) | Daily if on insulin; 2–3x/week if on SU |
| Fasting lipid profile | Annually |
| Serum creatinine + eGFR | Annually (6-monthly if CKD) |
| Urine albumin:creatinine ratio (ACR) | Annually |
| Dilated eye exam (retinopathy screen) | At diagnosis, then annually |
| Foot examination | Every visit (at least annually) |
| Blood pressure | Every visit |
| BMI / waist circumference | Every visit |

### 1.5 Referral Criteria

Refer to specialist (endocrinologist/diabetologist) when:
- HbA1c > 9% despite optimised therapy
- Recurrent severe hypoglycaemia
- Suspected type 1 diabetes or LADA
- Pregnancy (pre-conception counselling or gestational diabetes)
- Renal impairment (eGFR < 30)
- Diabetic foot ulcers (podiatrist + multidisciplinary)
- Proliferative retinopathy (ophthalmologist — urgent)

## 2. Hypertension — SASHA Guidelines

**Source**: South African Hypertension Practice Guideline 2014, Southern African Hypertension Society (SASHA). PHC STG Chapter 4: Cardiovascular Conditions (2024 update).

### 2.1 Diagnostic Criteria

| Classification | Systolic (mmHg) | Diastolic (mmHg) |
|---------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Optimal | < 120 | < 80 |
| Normal | 120–129 | 80–84 |
| High-normal | 130–139 | 85–89 |
| Grade 1 Hypertension | 140–159 | 90–99 |
| Grade 2 Hypertension | 160–179 | 100–109 |
| Grade 3 Hypertension | >= 180 | >= 110 |
| Isolated systolic HT | >= 140 | < 90 |

**Diagnosis**: Requires elevated BP on at least 3 separate occasions, 1–2 weeks apart. Ambulatory BP monitoring (ABPM) or home BP monitoring recommended to exclude white-coat hypertension.

### 2.2 BP Targets

| Patient Group | Target BP |
|--------------|-----------|
| General (uncomplicated) | < 140/90 mmHg |
| Diabetes mellitus | < 140/85 mmHg |
| CKD with proteinuria | < 130/80 mmHg |
| Elderly (> 80 years) | < 150/90 mmHg |
| Post-stroke | < 140/90 mmHg |

### 2.3 Step Therapy Algorithm

**STEP 1 — Monotherapy (First-Line)**

Choice depends on patient demographics and comorbidities:

| Patient Group | Preferred First-Line |
|--------------|---------------------|
| Black patients (any age) | CCB (amlodipine 5–10 mg) OR thiazide-like diuretic (hydrochlorothiazide 12.5–25 mg) |
| Non-black patients < 55 years | ACE-i (enalapril 10–20 mg) or ARB (losartan 50–100 mg) |
| Non-black patients >= 55 years | CCB or thiazide-like diuretic |
| Diabetes / CKD with proteinuria | ACE-i or ARB (mandatory) |
| Heart failure | ACE-i or ARB + beta-blocker |

- **Note**: ACE-i and ARB are less effective as monotherapy in black patients due to lower renin levels
- **Review at 4–8 weeks**: If not at target, proceed to Step 2

**STEP 2 — Dual Therapy**

Preferred combinations:
- ACE-i/ARB + CCB (most evidence-based)
- ACE-i/ARB + thiazide-like diuretic
- CCB + thiazide-like diuretic

**NEVER combine**: ACE-i + ARB (increased adverse effects, no benefit)

**STEP 3 — Triple Therapy**
- ACE-i/ARB + CCB + thiazide-like diuretic
- This is the standard triple combination before declaring resistant hypertension

**STEP 4 — Resistant Hypertension** (uncontrolled on triple therapy at adequate doses)
- Confirm adherence and exclude secondary causes
- Add spironolactone 25–50 mg/day (most evidence for 4th-line agent)
- Alternatives: alpha-blocker (doxazosin), beta-blocker (atenolol), or vasodilator (hydralazine)
- Refer to hypertension specialist

**Initiate combination therapy ab initio** if:
- BP >= 160/100 mmHg
- BP >= 20/10 mmHg above target

### 2.4 Monitoring Schedule

| Assessment | Frequency |
|-----------|-----------|
| BP measurement | Every visit (at least monthly until controlled, then 3–6 monthly) |
| Serum creatinine, eGFR, electrolytes | Baseline, 2 weeks after starting/changing ACE-i/ARB/diuretic, then annually |
| Urine dipstick (protein) | Baseline, then annually |
| Fasting glucose, lipid profile | Baseline, then annually |
| ECG | Baseline (LVH screening) |
| Fundoscopy | Baseline and if Grade 2/3 HT |

### 2.5 Referral Criteria

- Suspected secondary hypertension (young patient, resistant HT, hypokalaemia)
- Hypertensive emergency (BP > 180/120 with acute organ damage)
- Resistant hypertension (uncontrolled on 3+ drugs including a diuretic)
- Pregnancy with hypertension
- CKD with declining eGFR

## 3. Asthma — SA Thoracic Society / GINA-Adapted

**Source**: SA Thoracic Society (SATS) Position Statement 2021; GINA 2024 Strategy adapted for SA context. PHC STG Chapter 8: Respiratory Conditions.

### 3.1 Diagnostic Criteria

- Variable expiratory airflow limitation + history of respiratory symptoms (wheeze, shortness of breath, chest tightness, cough)
- Spirometry: FEV1/FVC ratio < 0.75–0.80 in adults
- Bronchodilator reversibility: increase in FEV1 > 200 mL AND > 12% from baseline after 200–400 mcg salbutamol
- Peak expiratory flow (PEF) variability > 10% (adults)

### 3.2 Asthma Control Assessment

| Feature | Well Controlled | Partly Controlled | Uncontrolled |
|---------|----------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Daytime symptoms | <= 2 days/week | > 2 days/week | |
| Night waking | None | Any | |
| Reliever use | <= 2 days/week | > 2 days/week | 3 or more features of partly controlled |
| Activity limitation | None | Any | |

### 3.3 Step Therapy — Track 1 (Preferred, GINA 2024)

**STEP 1 — Intermittent / Mild Asthma**
- As-needed low-dose ICS-formoterol (e.g., budesonide-formoterol 200/6 mcg PRN)
- NO daily controller needed
- Avoid SABA-only treatment (associated with increased exacerbation risk)

**STEP 2 — Mild Persistent Asthma**
- Low-dose ICS-formoterol as needed (maintenance and reliever — MART approach)
- Alternative: daily low-dose ICS + as-needed SABA

| ICS | Low Dose | Medium Dose | High Dose |
|-----|----------|-------------|-----------|
| Beclomethasone (MDI) | 100–200 mcg/day | 200–400 mcg/day | > 400 mcg/day |
| Budesonide (DPI) | 200–400 mcg/day | 400–800 mcg/day | > 800 mcg/day |
| Fluticasone propionate | 100–250 mcg/day | 250–500 mcg/day | > 500 mcg/day |

**STEP 3 — Moderate Persistent Asthma**
- Low-dose ICS-formoterol MART (maintenance and reliever therapy)
- Or: low-dose ICS-LABA maintenance + as-needed SABA
- Formoterol combinations: budesonide-formoterol 200/6 mcg 1 puff BD + PRN

**STEP 4 — Moderate-Severe Asthma**
- Medium-dose ICS-formoterol MART
- Or: medium-dose ICS-LABA + as-needed SABA
- Consider add-on: LAMA (tiotropium Respimat)
- Review inhaler technique and adherence before stepping up

**STEP 5 — Severe Asthma**
- High-dose ICS-LABA
- Add-on LAMA (tiotropium)
- Refer to specialist for phenotyping
- Consider biologic therapy (omalizumab for allergic asthma, mepolizumab/benralizumab for eosinophilic asthma)
- Low-dose oral corticosteroids as last resort only

### 3.4 Track 2 (Alternative — SABA as reliever)

Same step-up structure but uses SABA (salbutamol 200 mcg PRN) as reliever throughout, with ICS taken as a separate daily controller:
- Step 1: ICS whenever SABA is used
- Step 2: Daily low-dose ICS
- Step 3: Low-dose ICS-LABA
- Step 4: Medium/high-dose ICS-LABA +/- LAMA
- Step 5: Same as Track 1

### 3.5 Acute Exacerbation (Emergency)

- Salbutamol 4–8 puffs via spacer every 20 min for 1 hour
- Ipratropium bromide 4–8 puffs via spacer (severe cases)
- Oral prednisone 40–50 mg stat, continue for 5–7 days
- Oxygen to maintain SpO2 93–95%
- If no response: nebulised salbutamol 5 mg + ipratropium 0.5 mg, IV magnesium sulphate 2g over 20 min
- ICU admission if deteriorating

### 3.6 Monitoring

- Assess control at every visit using symptom questionnaire (ACT score)
- PEF monitoring for moderate-severe asthma
- Spirometry annually
- Step down after 3 months of good control
- Review inhaler technique at every visit

### 3.7 Referral Criteria

- Suspected occupational asthma
- Uncontrolled on Step 4 therapy (refer to pulmonologist)
- Life-threatening exacerbation
- Diagnostic uncertainty
- Pregnancy with poorly controlled asthma

## 4. HIV/AIDS — SA DoH ART Guidelines 2023

**Source**: National ART Clinical Guidelines 2023, SA Department of Health. Southern African HIV Clinicians Society Adult ART Guidelines 2023.

### 4.1 ART Initiation

- **Universal Test and Treat (UTT)**: All HIV-positive individuals should be initiated on ART regardless of CD4 count
- **Same-day initiation**: Offered to all patients who are ready, unless clinical reasons to defer (TB meningitis, cryptococcal meningitis — defer 4–6 weeks)
- **Baseline bloods**: CD4 count, viral load (VL), creatinine, FBC, ALT, HBsAg, RPR, urine pregnancy test (women of childbearing potential)

### 4.2 First-Line ART Regimen

**Adults and Adolescents (>= 20 kg)**:

| Regimen | Composition | Dose |
|---------|-------------|------|
| **TLD** (Preferred first-line) | Tenofovir (TDF) 300 mg + Lamivudine (3TC) 300 mg + Dolutegravir (DTG) 50 mg | 1 tablet daily |

**Special Populations**:

| Population | Regimen |
|-----------|---------|
| Children < 20 kg | Abacavir (ABC) + Lamivudine (3TC) + Dolutegravir (DTG) — ALD |
| Renal impairment (CrCl < 50) | Replace TDF with Abacavir (ABC) or Zidovudine (AZT) |
| TB co-infection on rifampicin | TLD + extra DTG 50 mg 12 hours apart (DTG BD dosing) |
| Hepatitis B co-infection | Must include TDF + 3TC or FTC in backbone (do NOT stop — risk of HBV flare) |

### 4.3 Second-Line ART

**After failing NNRTI-based first-line** (historical patients on TEE/TLE):
- Switch to **TLD** (TDF + 3TC + DTG)
- TDF may be safely recycled in second-line

**After failing DTG-based first-line** (TLD1 failure — rare):
- Genotypic resistance testing recommended
- Specialist consultation required
- Consider: AZT + 3TC + DTG (ALD2) or DRV/r-based regimen

**Third-Line ART**:
- Requires specialist/expert committee approval
- Darunavir/ritonavir (DRV/r) based + optimised backbone
- Based on genotypic resistance results

### 4.4 Viral Load Monitoring Schedule

| Timepoint | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| Baseline | VL at ART initiation (within 1 month) |
| 6 months | First VL check — target < 50 copies/mL |
| 12 months | Second VL check |
| Then annually | Routine VL monitoring |

**If VL elevated (>= 50 copies/mL)**:
1. Assess adherence (pill counts, pharmacy refill records, counselling)
2. Check for drug interactions, correct dosing, intercurrent infections
3. Repeat VL after 3 months of enhanced adherence counselling
4. If VL remains >= 1,000 copies/mL after 2nd check: consider treatment failure
5. Genotypic resistance testing before switching

**TLD1 vs TLD2 Classification**:
- **TLD1**: Patient on DTG-containing regimen, never failed a previous regimen
- **TLD2**: Patient on DTG-containing regimen after failing a previous (NNRTI-based) regimen

### 4.5 CD4 Monitoring

| Timepoint | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| Baseline | At ART initiation |
| Month 10/DC 10 | Repeat CD4 |
| Then 6-monthly | Until CD4 > 200 cells/mm3 |
| Once CD4 > 200 | Can discontinue CD4 monitoring if VL suppressed |

**CD4 < 200 cells/mm3**: Screen for opportunistic infections (TB, cryptococcal antigen, CMV retinitis)

### 4.6 Referral Criteria

- Confirmed virological failure on TLD (VL > 1,000 after adherence interventions)
- Third-line ART required
- Drug-resistant HIV
- Complex co-infections (TB meningitis, cryptococcal meningitis, Kaposi sarcoma)
- ART-related toxicity (renal impairment, hepatotoxicity, hypersensitivity)
- Pregnant women with VL > 1,000 at 32 weeks

## 5. Tuberculosis — SA DoH Guidelines

**Source**: SA National TB Guidelines; Updated RR-TB Clinical Guidelines September 2023; National TB Infection Treatment Guidelines 2023; WHO Consolidated Guidelines on TB (2024 update).

### 5.1 Diagnostic Criteria

- **GeneXpert MTB/RIF Ultra**: First-line diagnostic test in SA for all presumptive TB. Detects MTB + rifampicin resistance
- **Sputum microscopy (smear)**: Still used for monitoring but NOT for initial diagnosis
- **Chest X-ray**: Supportive but not diagnostic alone
- **Culture + DST**: Drug sensitivity testing for all rifampicin-resistant cases
- **TB-LAM**: Urine lateral flow lipoarabinomannan — for HIV+ patients with CD4 < 200

### 5.2 Drug-Sensitive TB Treatment

**Standard Regimen: 2HRZE / 4HR (6 months total)**

| Phase | Duration | Drugs | Dosing |
|-------|----------|-------|--------|
| **Intensive Phase** | 2 months | Rifampicin (R) + Isoniazid (H) + Pyrazinamide (Z) + Ethambutol (E) | Fixed-dose combination (FDC) daily |
| **Continuation Phase** | 4 months | Rifampicin (R) + Isoniazid (H) | FDC daily |

**Weight-based Dosing (Adults)**:

| Weight | RHZE (Intensive) | RH (Continuation) |
|--------|------------------|--------------------|
| 30–37 kg | 2 tablets daily | 2 tablets daily |
| 38–54 kg | 3 tablets daily | 3 tablets daily |
| 55–70 kg | 4 tablets daily | 4 tablets daily |
| > 70 kg | 5 tablets daily | 5 tablets daily |

**All treatment is directly observed (DOT/DOTS)**.

**Pyridoxine (Vitamin B6)**: 25 mg daily throughout treatment (prevents isoniazid-related peripheral neuropathy)

### 5.3 Drug-Resistant TB

**Rifampicin-Resistant / MDR-TB (Updated 2023)**:

| Regimen | Duration | Drugs |
|---------|----------|-------|
| **All-oral 9-month regimen** (standard) | 9 months | Bedaquiline + Levofloxacin/Moxifloxacin + Ethionamide + Linezolid + Isoniazid (high-dose) + Pyrazinamide + Ethambutol |
| **All-oral 6-month regimen** (BPaL) | 6 months | Bedaquiline + Pretomanid + Linezolid (+/- Moxifloxacin) |
| **Longer regimen** (FQ-resistant) | 18–20 months | Individualized based on DST |

**Key Changes in 2023 SA Guidelines**:
- Injectable agents (kanamycin, amikacin) largely replaced by all-oral regimens
- Bedaquiline-based regimens now standard of care
- Linezolid monitoring for myelosuppression and neuropathy essential

### 5.4 TB Preventive Therapy (TPT)

| Population | Regimen | Duration |
|-----------|---------|----------|
| HIV+ (all, regardless of TST) | 3HP (Isoniazid + Rifapentine weekly) | 12 weeks (12 doses) |
| HIV+ (alternative) | 6H (Isoniazid 300 mg daily) | 6 months |
| Household contacts (adults) | 3HP or 6H | As above |
| Children < 5 years (contacts) | 3RH (Rifampicin + Isoniazid daily) | 3 months |

### 5.5 Monitoring During Treatment

| Test | Timing |
|------|--------|
| Sputum for smear + culture | Baseline, month 2, month 5, end of treatment |
| Weight | Monthly |
| LFTs (ALT) | Baseline; repeat if symptoms of hepatotoxicity |
| Visual acuity (ethambutol) | Baseline, monthly during intensive phase |
| Audiometry (if aminoglycosides used) | Baseline, monthly |
| FBC (if linezolid used) | Baseline, then 2-weekly |
| ECG (if bedaquiline used) | Baseline, 2 weeks, monthly (QTc monitoring) |

### 5.6 Referral Criteria

- Rifampicin resistance detected on GeneXpert
- Treatment failure (culture positive at month 5)
- Drug-resistant TB (refer to DR-TB treatment site)
- Adverse drug reactions (hepatotoxicity, QTc prolongation, visual changes)
- TB meningitis, miliary TB, spinal TB (hospitalisation required)
- Treatment interruption > 2 months (restart assessment)

## 6. Mental Health — Depression and Anxiety

**Source**: SASOP (SA Society of Psychiatrists) Treatment Guidelines; PHC STG Chapter 16: Mental Health Conditions (2024 update); SA Depression and Anxiety Group (SADAG) recommendations.

### 6.1 Depression — Diagnostic Criteria (PHQ-9 Based)

At least 5 of the following symptoms, most days, for >= 2 weeks (must include #1 or #2):
1. Depressed mood
2. Loss of interest/pleasure (anhedonia)
3. Weight/appetite change
4. Insomnia or hypersomnia
5. Psychomotor agitation or retardation
6. Fatigue/loss of energy
7. Worthlessness/excessive guilt
8. Impaired concentration/indecisiveness
9. Suicidal ideation/plans

**Severity**: Mild (PHQ-9: 5–9), Moderate (10–14), Moderately severe (15–19), Severe (20–27)

### 6.2 Depression Step Therapy

**STEP 1 — Mild Depression**
- Psychoeducation and supportive counselling
- Exercise prescription (30 min moderate exercise 3–5x/week)
- Brief structured psychological interventions
- Monitor for 2–4 weeks before pharmacotherapy

**STEP 2 — Moderate Depression (First-Line Pharmacotherapy)**
- **Fluoxetine** (doctor-initiated at PHC): Start 20 mg alternate days for 2 weeks, then 20 mg daily
- **Citalopram** (alternative): Start 10 mg daily, increase to 20 mg after 1–2 weeks
- Maximum dose: Fluoxetine 60 mg/day, Citalopram 40 mg/day (20 mg in elderly)
- Allow 4–6 weeks for full therapeutic effect
- Monitor for initial agitation (first 2 weeks) — especially in adolescents/young adults
- **Always ask about suicidal ideation before initiating SSRIs** — refer first if active suicidal plans

**STEP 3 — Moderate-Severe Depression / SSRI Non-Response**
- Optimise SSRI dose to maximum
- Switch SSRI (e.g., fluoxetine to citalopram or sertraline)
- Consider amitriptyline 25–150 mg nocte (TCA — especially if insomnia/pain component)
- Combination: SSRI + low-dose amitriptyline (25 mg nocte for sleep)
- Add structured psychotherapy (CBT if available)

**STEP 4 — Severe / Treatment-Resistant Depression**
- Refer to psychiatrist
- Consider venlafaxine 75–225 mg/day (SNRI — doctor-initiated, hospital level)
- Mirtazapine 15–45 mg nocte (especially if weight loss, insomnia, nausea with SSRIs)
- Augmentation strategies (lithium, atypical antipsychotics — specialist only)
- Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) for severe refractory cases

### 6.3 Anxiety Disorders

**Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)**:
- First-line: SSRI (fluoxetine or citalopram) — same dosing as depression
- Duration: minimum 12–18 months for first episode
- Consider low-dose amitriptyline (25–50 mg nocte) if SSRI not tolerated

**Panic Disorder**:
- First-line: SSRI
- Short-term benzodiazepine (oxazepam 10–15 mg TDS) for acute phase — max 2 weeks
- Avoid long-term benzodiazepine use

**PTSD**:
- First-line: Fluoxetine or citalopram
- Refer for trauma-focused CBT or EMDR

### 6.4 Treatment Duration

| Scenario | Duration |
|----------|----------|
| First episode depression | 6–12 months from remission |
| Second episode | 2 years from remission |
| Third or subsequent episode | Consider lifelong treatment |
| First episode anxiety disorder | 12–18 months minimum |

### 6.5 Monitoring

- Review at 2 weeks (for side effects, suicidality assessment)
- Then monthly for first 3 months
- Then 3-monthly when stable
- PHQ-9 score at each visit
- Assess medication adherence

### 6.6 Referral Criteria

- Active suicidal ideation with plan (EMERGENCY — refer immediately)
- Psychotic features
- Bipolar disorder suspected
- No response to 2 adequate SSRI trials (6–8 weeks each at max dose)
- Substance use disorder comorbidity
- Pregnancy or breastfeeding (risk-benefit discussion with specialist)
- Children and adolescents (specialist initiation of SSRIs)

## 7. Antibiotic Prescribing Guidelines

**Source**: SA Pocket Guide to Antibiotic Prescribing (2014/2015); PHC STG Chapter 9: Infections; African Antibiotic Treatment Guidelines for Common Bacterial Infections (OneHealth Trust); SA Upper Respiratory Tract Infection Guidelines (2015 update).

### 7.1 Principles of Antibiotic Stewardship

- Use WHO AWaRe Classification:
  - **Access** antibiotics: first-choice for common infections (amoxicillin, metronidazole, doxycycline)
  - **Watch** antibiotics: higher resistance risk, reserve for specific indications (azithromycin, ciprofloxacin, ceftriaxone)
  - **Reserve** antibiotics: last resort (carbapenems, colistin, linezolid)
- Target: >= 60% of antibiotic prescriptions should be Access antibiotics

### 7.2 First-Line Antibiotics by Condition

#### Upper Respiratory Tract Infections

| Condition | First-Line | Alternative | Duration |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|----------|
| Acute pharyngitis/tonsillitis (Group A Strep) | Phenoxymethylpenicillin (Pen VK) 500 mg BD | Amoxicillin 500 mg BD; Erythromycin 500 mg BD (if penicillin allergic) | 10 days |
| Acute otitis media | Amoxicillin 500 mg TDS (adults); 90 mg/kg/day (children) | Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg BD | 5–7 days |
| Acute bacterial rhinosinusitis | Amoxicillin 500 mg TDS | Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg BD; Doxycycline 100 mg BD | 5–7 days |

**Important**: Most URTIs are viral — do NOT prescribe antibiotics for common cold, viral pharyngitis, or acute bronchitis without bacterial features.

#### Lower Respiratory Tract Infections

| Condition | First-Line | Alternative | Duration |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|----------|
| Community-acquired pneumonia (mild, outpatient) | Amoxicillin 500 mg–1 g TDS | Erythromycin 500 mg QDS (if penicillin allergic) | 5 days |
| CAP (moderate, >= 65 years or comorbidities) | Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg BD | Cefuroxime 500 mg BD | 5–7 days |
| CAP (severe, hospital admission) | Amoxicillin-clavulanate IV + Azithromycin 500 mg daily | Ceftriaxone 1–2 g IV daily + Azithromycin | 7–10 days |
| Acute exacerbation of COPD (purulent sputum) | Amoxicillin 500 mg TDS | Doxycycline 100 mg BD | 5 days |

#### Urinary Tract Infections

| Condition | First-Line | Alternative | Duration |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|----------|
| Uncomplicated cystitis (women) | Nitrofurantoin 100 mg BD | Fosfomycin 3 g single dose | 5 days (nitrofurantoin) |
| Uncomplicated cystitis (men) | Ciprofloxacin 500 mg BD | Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg BD | 7 days |
| Complicated UTI / pyelonephritis | Ciprofloxacin 500 mg BD | Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg BD; Ceftriaxone 1 g IV (severe) | 7–14 days |

#### Skin and Soft Tissue Infections

| Condition | First-Line | Alternative | Duration |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|----------|
| Impetigo (limited) | Topical mupirocin/fusidic acid | Flucloxacillin 500 mg QDS (extensive) | 5 days |
| Cellulitis (non-purulent) | Flucloxacillin 500 mg QDS | Clindamycin 300 mg QDS (penicillin allergy) | 5–7 days |
| Cellulitis with abscess (purulent) | Flucloxacillin 500 mg QDS + I&D | Clindamycin 300 mg QDS | 5–7 days |
| Erysipelas | Phenoxymethylpenicillin 500 mg QDS | Erythromycin 500 mg QDS | 5–7 days |

#### Gastrointestinal Infections

| Condition | First-Line | Alternative | Duration |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|----------|
| Acute bacterial diarrhoea (bloody/severe) | Ciprofloxacin 500 mg BD | Azithromycin 500 mg daily | 3 days |
| H. pylori eradication | Amoxicillin 1 g BD + Clarithromycin 500 mg BD + PPI | Metronidazole 400 mg BD (if penicillin allergy) + Clarithromycin + PPI | 14 days |
| Dental abscess | Amoxicillin 500 mg TDS + Metronidazole 400 mg TDS | Clindamycin 300 mg QDS | 5 days |

### 7.3 When NOT to Prescribe Antibiotics

- Common cold / viral URTI
- Acute bronchitis (viral in > 90% of cases)
- Viral pharyngitis (no exudate, no fever, Centor score < 3)
- Acute otitis media in children > 2 years with mild symptoms (watch and wait 48 hours)
- Uncomplicated acute gastroenteritis (most are viral or self-limiting)

## 8. Chronic Kidney Disease

**Source**: KDIGO 2024 Clinical Practice Guideline for CKD Evaluation and Management (SA-adapted); SA DoH PHC STG Chapter 7: Nephrological/Urological Disorders (2024); SA Renal Society Guidelines.

### 8.1 eGFR Staging

| Stage | eGFR (mL/min/1.73m2) | Description | Action Level |
|-------|----------------------|-------------|-------------|
| G1 | >= 90 | Normal or high | Monitor if risk factors present |
| G2 | 60–89 | Mildly decreased | Monitor if risk factors present |
| G3a | 45–59 | Mildly-moderately decreased | PHC management; annual monitoring |
| G3b | 30–44 | Moderately-severely decreased | PHC management; 6-monthly monitoring; referral recommended |
| G4 | 15–29 | Severely decreased | **Mandatory referral to nephrologist** |
| G5 | < 15 | Kidney failure | **Urgent referral**; plan for RRT or conservative care |

**Albuminuria Categories**:

| Category | Urine ACR (mg/g) | Description |
|----------|-------------------|-------------|
| A1 | < 30 | Normal to mildly increased |
| A2 | 30–300 | Moderately increased (microalbuminuria) |
| A3 | > 300 | Severely increased (macroalbuminuria) |

**CKD diagnosis requires**: eGFR < 60 OR albuminuria (ACR >= 30) OR structural abnormality, present for > 3 months.

### 8.2 eGFR Calculation

- Use **CKD-EPI 2021 equation** (creatinine-based; cystatin C for confirmation if needed)
- Do NOT use Cockcroft-Gault for staging (use for drug dosing only)
- eGFR reporting is mandatory on all creatinine results in SA

### 8.3 Treatment — First-Line Medications

**Three medication classes are first-line for CKD management**:

1. **RAASi (ACE-i or ARB)** — mandatory if albuminuria (ACR >= 30)
   - Enalapril 5–20 mg daily or Losartan 50–100 mg daily
   - Continue even if eGFR < 30 (do not stop for mild creatinine rise — tolerate up to 30% rise)
   - Monitor potassium (stop if K+ > 6.0 mmol/L)
   - Never combine ACE-i + ARB

2. **SGLT2 inhibitor** — recommended if eGFR >= 20
   - Dapagliflozin 10 mg daily or Empagliflozin 10 mg daily
   - Benefits: slows CKD progression, reduces proteinuria, CV protection
   - Initial eGFR dip (5–10%) is expected and NOT a reason to stop
   - Can initiate even without diabetes

3. **Statin therapy** — for all CKD patients >= stage G3
   - Atorvastatin 20 mg or Simvastatin 20–40 mg daily (avoid simvastatin with CYP3A4 inhibitors)

### 8.4 Additional Management

- **BP target**: < 130/80 (if albuminuria); < 140/90 (if no albuminuria)
- **Diabetes**: HbA1c target 7.0–8.0% if CKD stage 4–5 (avoid hypoglycaemia)
- **Anaemia**: If Hb < 10 g/dL, check iron studies. Start oral iron. Consider EPO if Hb < 9 and iron-replete (specialist)
- **Metabolite management**: Phosphate binders, active vitamin D, bicarbonate supplementation — specialist guided
- **Drug dose adjustment**: Review ALL medications for renal dosing (SAMF reference)
  - NSAIDs: AVOID in CKD
  - Metformin: Reduce dose if eGFR 30–45; STOP if < 30
  - Gabapentin, pregabalin, allopurinol: reduce dose per eGFR

### 8.5 Monitoring Schedule

| Test | Stage G1–G2 | Stage G3 | Stage G4 | Stage G5 |
|------|------------|----------|----------|----------|
| eGFR + creatinine | Annually | 6-monthly | 3-monthly | 1–3 monthly |
| Urine ACR | Annually | 6-monthly | 3-monthly | 3-monthly |
| Electrolytes (K+, Na+, Ca2+, PO4) | Annually | 6-monthly | 3-monthly | Monthly |
| Hb/FBC | Annually | 6-monthly | 3-monthly | Monthly |
| PTH | — | Annually | 6-monthly | 3-monthly |
| Bicarbonate | — | Annually | 3-monthly | Monthly |

### 8.6 Referral Criteria

Refer to nephrologist when:
- eGFR < 30 mL/min/1.73m2 (obligatory) OR eGFR < 60 (recommended if available)
- Persistent proteinuria (ACR > 300) with or without haematuria
- Rapidly declining eGFR (> 5 mL/min/year or > 25% decline in 12 months)
- Suspected glomerulonephritis (haematuria + proteinuria)
- Refractory hypertension (resistant to 3+ drugs) with CKD
- Hyperkalaemia (K+ > 6.0) or metabolic acidosis
- Abnormal kidney imaging (polycystic kidneys, hydronephrosis, renal mass)
- CKD in pregnancy
- CKD stage G5: discussion about renal replacement therapy (dialysis/transplant) vs conservative care

## 9. Cardiovascular Risk and Dyslipidaemia

**Source**: SA Dyslipidaemia Guideline Consensus Statement 2018 (SA Heart Association + LASSA); PHC STG Chapter 4: Cardiovascular Conditions (2024).

### 9.1 Cardiovascular Risk Stratification

**Automatic High/Very High Risk** (no scoring needed):

| Category | Conditions |
|----------|-----------|
| Very High Risk | Established CVD (previous MI, stroke, PAD), Diabetes + organ damage, Severe CKD (eGFR < 30), Familial hypercholesterolaemia with CVD |
| High Risk | Diabetes mellitus (without organ damage), Moderate CKD (eGFR 30–59), Single risk factor markedly elevated (e.g., TC > 8 mmol/L), Familial hypercholesterolaemia |

**For others**: Use Framingham Risk Score or SCORE chart adapted for SA
- Low risk: < 10% 10-year CVD risk
- Moderate risk: 10–20%
- High risk: > 20%

### 9.2 LDL-C Treatment Targets

| Risk Category | LDL-C Target | Additional |
|--------------|-------------|-----------|
| Low risk | < 3.0 mmol/L | Lifestyle modification first |
| Moderate risk | < 3.0 mmol/L | Lifestyle + consider statin |
| High risk | < 2.5 mmol/L | AND >= 50% reduction from baseline |
| Very high risk | < 1.8 mmol/L | AND >= 50% reduction from baseline |

### 9.3 Statin Therapy

**First-line**: Statin (HMG-CoA reductase inhibitor)

| Statin | Low-intensity (LDL reduction ~30%) | Moderate-intensity (~30–50%) | High-intensity (~50–60%) |
|--------|-----------------------------------|-------------------------------|--------------------------|
| Simvastatin | 10 mg | 20–40 mg | — |
| Atorvastatin | — | 10–20 mg | 40–80 mg |
| Rosuvastatin | — | 5–10 mg | 20–40 mg |

**PHC Level**: Simvastatin 10–40 mg daily (most widely available)
**Hospital Level**: Atorvastatin or Rosuvastatin for high/very high risk

**Statin intolerance**: Try alternate statin at lower dose, alternate-day dosing (atorvastatin or rosuvastatin only — long half-life), or ezetimibe 10 mg daily as alternative

**Contraindication**: Simvastatin contraindicated with protease inhibitors (HIV patients — use atorvastatin or rosuvastatin)

### 9.4 Non-Statin Lipid-Lowering Therapy

- **Ezetimibe** 10 mg daily: Add to statin if target not reached; LDL reduction 15–20%
- **PCSK9 inhibitors** (evolocumab, alirocumab): Specialist-initiated; for familial hypercholesterolaemia or very high risk patients not at target on maximal statin + ezetimibe
- **Fibrates** (bezafibrate, fenofibrate): For isolated hypertriglyceridaemia (TG > 5 mmol/L); caution with statin combinations

### 9.5 Aspirin for Primary Prevention

- **NOT routinely recommended** for primary prevention (risk of bleeding outweighs benefit in most patients)
- Consider aspirin 75–100 mg daily if:
  - 10-year CVD risk > 20% AND low bleeding risk
  - Diabetes with multiple additional risk factors
- **Secondary prevention**: Aspirin 75–100 mg daily mandatory for all patients with established CVD (MI, stroke, PAD)

### 9.6 Monitoring

| Test | Frequency |
|------|-----------|
| Fasting lipid profile | Baseline; 6–8 weeks after starting/changing statin; then annually |
| ALT | Baseline; 6–8 weeks after starting statin (check for hepatotoxicity); then annually |
| CK (creatine kinase) | Baseline; only repeat if muscle symptoms (myalgia, weakness) |
| Fasting glucose | Annually (statins may increase diabetes risk slightly) |

### 9.7 Referral Criteria

- Suspected familial hypercholesterolaemia (TC > 7.5 mmol/L or LDL > 5.0, tendon xanthomata, family history of premature CVD)
- Severe hypertriglyceridaemia (TG > 10 mmol/L — risk of pancreatitis)
- Statin intolerance (failed 2+ statins) — refer to lipidologist
- LDL target not achieved on maximal therapy — consider PCSK9 inhibitor (specialist)

## 10. National STG and Essential Medicines List (STG/EML)

**Source**: Primary Healthcare Standard Treatment Guidelines and Essential Medicines List for South Africa, 8th Edition (2024); Adult Hospital Level STG/EML 6th Edition (2024); Paediatric Hospital Level STG/EML (2023). Published by SA National Department of Health.

### 10.1 Overview

The STG/EML is the **definitive reference** for clinically appropriate treatment in South Africa's public health sector. It is used:
- As the standard against which prescribing is audited
- By medical schemes to determine PMB (Prescribed Minimum Benefits) treatment standards
- For formulary development by hospitals and medical schemes
- By nurse prescribers (all listed medicines can be prescribed by authorised nurse prescribers except "doctor prescribed" or "doctor initiated" items)

### 10.2 Structure

|-------|---------|
| **PHC Level** (Clinics, CHCs) | 8th Edition 2024 — covers conditions manageable at primary care level |

| **Paediatric Hospital Level** | 2023 Edition — children-specific conditions |

| **Specialist initiated** | Hospital specialist initiates; continued at PHC |

### 10.5 Essential Medicines for Claims Validation

For claims adjudication, the STG/EML determines:
1. **Appropriate drug for the diagnosis**: Is this the recommended first-line treatment?
2. **Correct dose and duration**: Does the prescription match STG dosing?
3. **Step therapy compliance**: Was the first-line agent tried before escalation?
4. **Prescribing authority**: Was the medicine prescribed by an appropriately qualified practitioner?
5. **PMB coverage**: Conditions on the Chronic Disease List (CDL) must be treated according to STG

## Quick Reference: Claims Validation Decision Matrix

Use this matrix to validate whether a claim's treatment is clinically appropriate:

| Check | Pass Criteria | Red Flag |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **Diagnosis matches treatment** | Drug is listed in STG for the claimed ICD-10 code | Drug has no indication for the diagnosis |
| **First-line tried first** | Patient received first-line agent before escalation | Jump to second/third-line without documented first-line failure |
| **Dose within range** | Dose matches STG recommended range | Dose exceeds maximum or is sub-therapeutic |
| **Duration appropriate** | Treatment duration matches STG (e.g., 5 days antibiotics) | Excessive duration (e.g., 30 days antibiotics for UTI) |
| **Step therapy followed** | Evidence of step-up (e.g., metformin before SU before insulin) | Insulin initiated without oral agent trial |
| **Specialist medication at correct level** | "Specialist-initiated" meds have specialist consultation documented | Specialist-level drugs prescribed at PHC without referral |
| **Monitoring done** | Required blood tests ordered (e.g., HbA1c for diabetes, VL for HIV) | No monitoring for chronic medications |
| **Referral criteria met** | Referral to specialist when guidelines indicate | No referral despite meeting criteria (e.g., eGFR < 30) |
