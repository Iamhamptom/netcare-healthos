## 3. Regulatory Landscape

### 3.1 Medical Schemes Act 131 of 1998

The foundational legislation governing medical scheme claims processing is the Medical Schemes Act, particularly **Section 59**, which establishes the 30-day payment obligation:

> *"A medical scheme shall... pay to a member or a supplier of service, any benefit owing to that member or supplier of service within 30 days after the day on which the claim in respect of such benefit was received by the medical scheme."* — Section 59(2)

**Regulation 6** further specifies:
- Claims must be submitted within **120 days** of the date of service
- Schemes must pay or notify rejection within **30 days** of receipt
- If rejected, providers have **60 days** to correct and resubmit
- If the scheme fails to notify within 30 days, the **burden of proof shifts to the scheme**

**Regulation 8** on Prescribed Minimum Benefits (PMBs) establishes that:
- Any benefit option must pay in full, without co-payment or deductibles, the diagnosis, treatment, and care costs of PMB conditions
- At a Designated Service Provider (DSP): pay in full
- No DSP appointed: must pay in full at any provider (*CMS v Genesis* [2015] ZASCA 161)
- PMBs must be funded from the risk pool, never from savings accounts

### 3.2 POPIA Health Regulations 2026

The Protection of Personal Information Act (POPIA) Health Regulations came into force on **27 February 2026** with **no grace period**. Health data is classified as "special personal information" under Section 26, and its processing is prohibited by default unless a specific exception applies.

Key implications for health-tech companies:
- Explicit consent required for processing health data beyond direct treatment purposes
- Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) mandatory for AI/ML systems processing health data
- Cross-border transfer restrictions for cloud-hosted health data
- Breach notification within 72 hours to the Information Regulator
- Data minimisation principle: collect only what is necessary for the stated purpose

### 3.3 HPCSA Booklet 20 — AI as Decision Support

The Health Professions Council of South Africa (HPCSA) Ethical Booklet 20 establishes that AI systems in healthcare must function as **decision-support tools only**. The registered healthcare practitioner retains final clinical responsibility for all diagnostic and treatment decisions, regardless of AI recommendations. This positions VisioCorp's claims intelligence appropriately: our AI assists, validates, and suggests — it does not override clinical judgment.

### 3.4 SAHPRA Software as a Medical Device (SaMD)

The South African Health Products Regulatory Authority (SAHPRA) classifies AI/ML software on a four-tier system:

| Class | Risk Level | Example |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| A | Lowest | Billing and administrative software |
| B | Low-moderate | Clinical decision support (non-critical) |
| C | Moderate-high | Diagnostic AI influencing treatment decisions |
| D | Highest | Autonomous diagnostic/treatment systems |

VisioCorp's claims intelligence platform — focused on billing validation, coding assistance, and rejection prediction — is appropriately classified as **Class A-B**, with an ISO 13485 compliance deadline of **1 April 2028** for Class B and above.

### 3.5 NHI Act and Constitutional Challenge

The **National Health Insurance Act 20 of 2023**, signed by President Ramaphosa on 15 May 2024, envisions a single-payer NHI Fund with medical schemes restricted to complementary cover only. However, 12+ constitutional challenges are pending, with the **Constitutional Court hearing scheduled for 5-7 May 2026**. Full NHI implementation is not expected before **~2035**.

Regardless of the ConCourt outcome, the direction toward digital health infrastructure, FHIR-based interoperability, and standardised claims processing is irreversible. Technology vendors must build NHI-ready systems regardless of the legislative timeline.

### 3.6 Section 59 Investigation: Racial Discrimination in FWA Systems

In April 2025, an investigation chaired by Advocate Tembeka Ngcukaitobi found that **Discovery Health, GEMS, and Medscheme** had engaged in **systemic racial discrimination** against Black healthcare providers in their fraud, waste, and abuse investigations (2012-2019). Location-based scoring algorithms created **2-3x higher investigation probability** for township and rural practices.

This finding has profound implications for health-tech companies building AI claims systems: all algorithmic models must incorporate bias testing and fairness constraints. VisioCorp's fraud detection architecture includes peer comparison adjustments for practice location, patient demographics, and socioeconomic factors to ensure equitable treatment.

South Africa's claims infrastructure currently operates on the **EDIFACT MEDCLM v0-912-13.4** messaging standard — a legacy format that limits interoperability and innovation. The global health IT industry has moved toward **FHIR (Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources) R4**, which is explicitly mandated by South Africa's National Digital Health Strategy.

| Standard | Status |
|----------|--------|

### 4.3 The SA FHIR Implementation Guide Gap

South Africa **does not have** an officially published, government-endorsed FHIR Implementation Guide on the HL7 FHIR registry (registry.fhir.org). This is in stark contrast to comparable markets:

- **United States**: US Core (hl7.org/fhir/us/core)
- **Australia**: AU Base (hl7.org.au/fhir/core)
- **United Kingdom**: UK Core (simplifier.net/HL7FHIRUKCoreR4)
- **South Africa**: None published

This represents a **first-mover opportunity of extraordinary value**. Whoever defines and publishes the de facto South African FHIR profiles controls the vocabulary of the national health interoperability ecosystem. VisioCorp is positioned to fill this gap.

## 8. NHI Readiness

### 8.1 NHI Fund IT Architecture

The NHI Act (Sections 36-42) requires the establishment of comprehensive digital systems: a Beneficiary Registry, Provider Registry, Claims Management System, Fraud Management System, Health Information System, and Payment System. The **CSIR (Council for Scientific and Industrial Research)** has been appointed as the primary technology partner, with the **OpenHIE architecture** as the reference framework.

The **Health Patient Registration System (HPRS)** — the first visible NHI technology deliverable — has been developed by CSIR but has not yet reached national scale. As of March 2026, no NHI Fund board has been appointed, no CEO has been hired, and no provider contracts have been signed.

### 8.2 DRG Payment Model

The NHI is expected to adopt a **Diagnosis-Related Group (DRG)** payment model for hospital services, adapted from the Australian AR-DRG classification. Under DRG, hospitals receive a fixed payment per admission based on diagnosis, procedures, patient demographics, and complications — rather than fee-for-service billing.

Key implications:
- **Coding accuracy becomes even more critical**: DRG assignment is entirely dependent on correct ICD-10 and procedure coding
- **Upcoding risk shifts from individual claims to case-mix**: hospitals will be incentivised to assign more complex DRGs
- **VisioCorp's coding intelligence** positions practices for DRG-based billing from day one

### 8.3 Capitation Model for Primary Care

NHI primary care is expected to operate on a **capitation model**, where contracted GPs receive a fixed per-capita payment for a registered patient population (estimated 2,000-10,000 patients per doctor). The expected allocation split is approximately **70/20/10** (primary care / specialist referral / hospital admission).

This requires fundamentally different technology:
- Patient registration and roster management
- Population health analytics
- Risk adjustment (age, sex, chronic disease burden)
- Quality metrics and pay-for-performance tracking

### 8.4 VisioCorp's Dual-Mode Architecture

VisioCorp is building a **dual-mode claims engine** that supports both current fee-for-service billing and future NHI payment models:

| Mode | Current | NHI Future |

| **Claims format** | EDIFACT MEDCLM | FHIR R4 |
| **Adjudication** | Scheme-specific rules | NHI Fund standardised |
| **Provider identification** | BHF practice number | HPRS-registered provider |
| **Beneficiary identification** | Scheme membership number | NHI beneficiary number (SA ID-linked) |
