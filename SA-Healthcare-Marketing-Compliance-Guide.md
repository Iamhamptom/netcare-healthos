- **T26-T28**: Burns of eye, respiratory tract, internal organs
- **T36-T50**: Poisoning by specific drug categories
- **T51-T65**: Toxic effects (alcohol, solvents, pesticides, metals, gases)
- **T66-T78**: Effects of radiation, heat, hypothermia, air pressure, asphyxiation, maltreatment, anaphylaxis

## 2.4 SA-Specific Coding Rules

### Master Industry Table (MIT)
- The **SA ICD-10 MIT** is the ONLY official reference list for ICD-10 codes in SA
- Maintained by the National Department of Health
- Includes `Valid_ICD-10_Clinical Use` and `Valid_ICD-10_Primary` flags
- [Download from health.gov.za](https://www.health.gov.za/icd-10-master-industry-table/)

### Key SA Differences from WHO Standard
1. Where WHO uses "optional", SA replaces with **"mandatory"** (External Cause Codes are mandatory)
2. SA uses WHO vanilla ICD-10, NOT US ICD-10-CM
3. No 7th character extension (A/D/S) — SA uses T90-T98 sequelae block instead
4. All codes must be used to **highest level of specificity**
5. Using a 3-character code when 4-5 character subcodes exist is **non-compliant**

### MANDATORY: External Cause Code (ECC) Pairing

**Every S-code or T-code must be accompanied by an External Cause Code (V/W/X/Y) in the secondary position. Claims without an ECC WILL NOT be reimbursed.**

```
Primary Diagnosis (PDX):    T-code (the injury/poisoning)
Secondary Diagnosis (SDX):  V/W/X/Y code (the external cause)
```

### External Cause Code Categories (Chapter XX)
| Block | Description |
|---|---|
| V01-V99 | Transport accidents |
| W00-W19 | Falls |
| W20-W49 | Exposure to inanimate mechanical forces |
| W50-W64 | Exposure to animate mechanical forces |
| W65-W74 | Accidental drowning |
| X00-X09 | Smoke, fire, flames |
| X40-X49 | Accidental poisoning |
| X60-X84 | Intentional self-harm |
| X85-Y09 | Assault |
| Y40-Y84 | Complications of medical/surgical care |
| Y90-Y98 | Supplementary factors |

### Council for Medical Schemes (CMS) Requirements
1. All providers must include ICD-10 codes on all claims
2. No ICD-10 code is reserved for a specific discipline
3. Fraudulent coding (changing codes to PMB list) is **fraud**
4. Only the treating clinician may assign ICD-10 codes

## 2.5 Medical Aid Requirements

All SA medical schemes require:
- ICD-10 codes on all claims
- External Cause Codes with all S/T injury codes
- Codes from the SA MIT
- Maximum specificity

| Scheme | Key Note |
|---|---|
| **Discovery Health** | Without codes, injury claims may pay from day-to-day benefits instead of PMB |
| **GEMS** | Ambulatory PMB claims auto-paid as PMB with correct ICD-10 — no pre-auth needed |
| **Momentum** | Publishes PMB booklet mapping ICD-10 to PMB conditions |
| **Bonitas** | Claims without ECC on injury codes will be rejected |
| **All schemes** | Cannot reimburse when ECC is missing from injury codes |

# 3. Marketing Laws & Rules

## 3.1 HPCSA Ethical Rules on Advertising

Key documents: **Booklet 2** (Ethical Rules, Rules 3-8), **Booklet 3** (Making Professional Services Known), **Booklet 11** (Over-servicing, March 2025), **Booklet 16** (Social Media, August 2019).

### Core Rule (Rule 3)
A practitioner **SHALL be allowed** to advertise services PROVIDED the advertisement is:
- Not unprofessional
- Not untruthful
- Not deceptive or misleading
- Does not cause consumers unwarranted anxiety about health conditions

### Canvassing (PROHIBITED)
Drawing attention to personal qualities, superior knowledge, quality of service, professional guarantees, or best practice.

### Touting (PROHIBITED)
Drawing attention to offers, guarantees, or material benefits outside professional service categories to entice the public.

### Practice Names & Signage (Rule 5)
- Must use practitioner's own name or registered partner(s) name
- **Descriptive trade names PROHIBITED** (e.g., "Best Smile Dental" not allowed)
- Signs may display: name, qualifications (abbreviated), specialty, practice number, contact details, consulting hours
- Logos may NOT be used on outside signs
- Notices on road signposts, dustbins, cars are NOT permissible
- May NOT claim "membership" of HPCSA (it is registration, not membership)

### Website Content
**Permitted:** Name, qualifications, specialty, practice location, contact details, consulting hours, services offered (factual), educational health info, practice photos (premises only), factual professional bio.

**Prohibited:** Claims of superiority, guaranteed outcomes, patient testimonials, unlawful health/curative claims, unregistered qualifications, content creating health anxiety.

### Social Media (Booklet 16)
- Written informed consent before publishing ANY patient information
- Do NOT interact with patients via social media (professional boundaries)
- Never endorse hospitals/facilities/products for financial gain
- Everything posted may be copied, reproduced, shared — remains in perpetuity

### Patient Testimonials — EFFECTIVELY PROHIBITED
- Constitute canvassing (drawing attention to quality of service)
- Posting, sharing, or soliciting patient reviews is prohibited
- Patients may post on third-party platforms voluntarily, but practitioner must not solicit, share, or use these

### Before-and-After Photos — HIGHLY RESTRICTED
- Can constitute canvassing (implying superior skill)
- Create misleading expectations
- Require explicit written consent (POPIA + HPCSA)
- Should generally be avoided in marketing
- For clinical journal use: full consent mandatory, de-identified

### Fee Advertising (Rule 7)
- Must inform patients upfront of fees with written evidence
- Advertising fees is permitted if accurate and not misleading
- Must not competitively undercut or disparage other practitioners

### Specialist Titles (Rule 4)
- May NOT advertise unregistered qualifications alongside registered ones
- Only HPCSA-registered specialists may use specialist titles
- Claiming unregistered qualifications is a **criminal offence**

## 3.2 Key Legislation

### Health Professions Act (Act 56 of 1974)
- Parent legislation for HPCSA
- Section 49 empowers HPCSA to make conduct rules
- Criminal offences: practicing without registration, falsely implying registration, claiming false qualifications

### National Health Act (Act 61 of 2003)
- Requires appropriate information dissemination on health services
- Establishes patient rights to information, confidentiality, informed consent

### Consumer Protection Act (CPA, Act 68 of 2008)
- Applies to ALL suppliers including medical practitioners
- **Direct marketing time restrictions:**
  - Weekdays: 08:00-20:00 only
  - Saturdays: 09:00-13:00 only
  - Sundays and public holidays: **NO marketing contact**
- Consumers have free opt-out rights at any time
- Where CPA is more permissive than HPCSA, the **stricter HPCSA rules apply**

### POPIA (Act 4 of 2013)
- Health information = **Special Personal Information** (highest protection)
- Processing prohibited by default unless specific conditions met
- **Non-existing patients:** May contact ONCE to seek consent; without consent, no further marketing
- **Existing patients:** May market SIMILAR services unless opted out
- Explicit consent required for: patient health info in marketing, before/after photos, testimonials, sharing data with third parties
- **NEW (March 2026):** Regulations on Processing Health Information gazetted (GG 54268)

### Medicines and Related Substances Act (Act 101 of 1965)
- Administered by SAHPRA
- Schedule 0 (OTC): may advertise to public
- Schedules 2-6 (prescription): may ONLY advertise to authorised prescribers
- **Public advertising of prescription medicines is PROHIBITED**
- False advertising of health products: fine or up to **10 years imprisonment**

### Medical Schemes Act (Act 131 of 1998)
- Criminal offence to market as a medical scheme when not registered
- False claims for medical scheme benefits are criminal
- Must NOT imply or guarantee medical aid coverage unless verified

### Advertising Regulatory Board (ARB) / ASA
- ARB superseded ASA in November 2018
- Advertisements must be accurate, complete, clear
- Must not mislead, disparage competitors, or imitate other ads
- No gifts to HCPs as inducement

## 3.3 Digital Marketing Rules

### Google Ads — YES, with restrictions
- Content must comply with HPCSA rules
- Must be factual, no superiority claims, no testimonials
- Compliant: "Dr. Smith - ENT Practice, Sandton - Book Online"
- Non-compliant: "Best ENT surgeon in Johannesburg - Guaranteed results"

### Facebook/Instagram Ads — YES, with restrictions
- Factual service advertising permitted
- Cannot use testimonials, before/after images, outcome guarantees
- Educational content, practice info, and health awareness campaigns work well

### Email Marketing
- Existing patients: may email for similar services unless opted out
- New contacts: must obtain opt-in consent (one initial contact permitted)
- Every email needs free opt-out mechanism

### SMS Marketing
- **Weekdays 08:00-20:00, Saturdays 09:00-13:00**
- **Sundays/public holidays: PROHIBITED**
- Opt-in consent required; must include STOP reply capability
- Appointment reminders are clinical (not marketing) — permitted without marketing consent

### SEO and Online Directories
- SEO is fully permitted and recommended
- Avoid keyword stuffing with "best doctor" claims
- Directory listings (Medpages, RecoMed, SA Medical Specialists) permitted with factual info
- Do not solicit reviews

## 3.4 Penalties

| Body | Penalty |
|---|---|
| **HPCSA** | Reprimand, fine, suspension 1 month to 10 years, permanent removal from register |
| **POPIA** | Up to R10 million fine; 1-10 years imprisonment |
| **CPA** | Up to R1 million or 10% of annual turnover |
| **SAHPRA** | Fine or up to 10 years imprisonment |
| **Medical Schemes Act** | Criminal offences for false claims |

## 3.5 Practical Do's and Don'ts

### DO
1. Professional, mobile-friendly website with factual practice information
2. Use SEO for discoverability
3. List on recognised medical directories
4. Run compliant Google/social media ads (factual, professional)
5. Share educational health content (non-promotional)
6. Display registered qualifications and specialty
7. Inform patients upfront about fees with written schedules
8. Obtain explicit written consent before using any patient information
9. Include opt-out mechanisms in all marketing
10. Maintain consent records
11. POPIA-compliant privacy notice on website and in practice
12. Separate clinical from marketing communications

### DON'T
1. Patient testimonials, reviews, or endorsements in marketing
2. Before-and-after photos for promotion
3. "Best," "number one," "top-rated" or superiority claims
4. Guarantee treatment outcomes
5. Non-medical incentives to attract patients (free WiFi, coffee to attract)
6. Descriptive trade names for practice
7. Ads on road signs, dustbins, cars
8. Logos on outside signs
9. Advertise prescription medicines to the public
10. Claim unregistered qualifications or specialties
11. Endorse products for financial gain
12. Marketing SMS on Sundays/holidays/outside permitted hours
13. Marketing without opt-in consent or opt-out mechanisms
14. Share patient data with third parties without consent
15. Create health anxiety to drive appointments
16. Imply/guarantee medical aid coverage unless verified

# 4. Compliance Checklist

## 4.1 HPCSA Registration Compliance

- [ ] Confirm practitioner holds current, valid HPCSA registration before marketing goes live
- [ ] Verify practice number and include in all formal communications
- [ ] For specialists: confirm registered in relevant specialty with HPCSA
- [ ] Confirm scope of practice — all marketed services must fall within scope
- [ ] Do not market services the practitioner is not registered to provide

## 4.2 Patient Privacy & Data Protection (POPIA)

### Consent
- [ ] Explicit, informed, voluntary consent before using patient data for marketing
- [ ] Marketing consent SEPARATE from treatment consent (not bundled)
- [ ] Patients can withdraw consent at any time, free of charge
- [ ] Never use patient images/testimonials/case studies without specific written consent
- [ ] Maintain a consent register (when, scope, withdrawals)

### Data Retention
| Record Type | Retention Period |
|---|---|
| Patient clinical records | 6 years from dormancy |
| Minor patient records | Until patient turns 21 |
| Mentally incompetent patient records | Patient's lifetime |
| Occupational health records | 20 years |
| Hazardous exposure records | 25 years |
| Marketing consent records | Duration of relationship + buffer |
| Advertising copies | 3 years (CPA) |
| Complaint records | 6 years minimum |

### Cross-Border Data Transfer
- [ ] Do not transfer patient data outside SA unless recipient country has adequate data protection
- [ ] If using cloud-hosted marketing tools (Mailchimp, HubSpot), verify data storage location
- [ ] Prior authorisation from Information Regulator may apply for health data transfers

### Information Officer
- [ ] Appoint Information Officer (typically practice owner) and register with Information Regulator
- [ ] Appoint at least one Deputy Information Officer
- [ ] Publish Information Officer contact details on practice website

### Patient Communication
- [ ] All marketing communications require prior opt-in consent
- [ ] Every marketing message must include clear opt-out/unsubscribe
- [ ] Process opt-out requests within 5 business days
- [ ] Maintain suppression list of opted-out contacts
- [ ] Do not include marketing content in clinical communications without separate consent

## 4.3 Content Compliance

- [ ] All treatment claims must be truthful, accurate, and verifiable
- [ ] Claims supported by credible scientific evidence
- [ ] No off-label promotion of medicines or devices
- [ ] No prohibited superlatives ("best", "leading", "top", "guaranteed")
- [ ] No fear-based messaging creating unwarranted anxiety
- [ ] No comparative claims against other practitioners
- [ ] Images must not be misleading; anatomical drawings must be accurate
- [ ] Before/after photos: explicit consent, no digital manipulation, clear labelling
- [ ] Do not use stock images implying they are real patients

## 4.4 Digital Platform Compliance

### Website
- [ ] Medical disclaimer (information is not substitute for professional advice)
- [ ] POPIA privacy policy (data collected, how used, who shared with, patient rights)
- [ ] Display HPCSA registration number and practice number
- [ ] Display Information Officer contact details
- [ ] Consent checkbox (not pre-ticked) on data collection forms
- [ ] Terms and Conditions (limitation of liability, governing law)
- [ ] ECTA compliance: full business name, physical address, contact details, registration numbers

### Online Booking
- [ ] POPIA-compliant data collection and storage
- [ ] Clear cancellation/rescheduling/no-show policies
- [ ] Booking platform provider has signed Data Processing Addendum
- [ ] Data transmission encrypted (SSL/TLS minimum)

### Telemedicine (Booklet 10)
- [ ] Only market telehealth if practitioner is HPCSA-registered and compliant with HPCSA Telehealth Guidelines (Dec 2021)
- [ ] Same standards as face-to-face consultations
- [ ] Do not market treatments based solely on online questionnaires
- [ ] Clearly state limitations of telehealth

### Reviews & Ratings
- [ ] Do not incentivise, pay for, or fabricate patient reviews
- [ ] Do not selectively solicit only positive reviews
- [ ] Never disclose patient information when responding to negative reviews
- [ ] Monitor third-party platforms for inaccurate claims

## 4.5 ENT Practice-Specific Compliance

### Hearing Aid Marketing
- [ ] Hearing aids classified as medical devices — regulated by SAHPRA
- [ ] Do not make claims beyond manufacturer's SAHPRA-registered data
- [ ] Do not advertise "free hearing tests" as loss-leader for device sales (touting)
- [ ] Comply with SAMED Code of Ethical Marketing
- [ ] Pricing must be transparent (fitting fees, follow-up costs, warranty)

### Cosmetic Procedures (Rhinoplasty, etc.)
- [ ] Do not guarantee cosmetic outcomes
- [ ] Before/after images require explicit consent, no digital alteration
- [ ] Do not use social media influencers to promote procedures (touting)
- [ ] Clearly state risks, recovery time, and limitations
- [ ] Verify practitioner is registered in relevant specialty
- [ ] Do not create undue pressure via financing offers

### Audiological Services
- [ ] Audiologists must be HPCSA-registered
- [ ] Clearly distinguish diagnostic services from hearing aid sales
- [ ] Do not imply audiological screening replaces ENT consultation
- [ ] Paediatric services: avoid creating parental anxiety

## 4.6 Insurance & Liability

- [ ] Professional indemnity insurance is **mandatory** for all HPCSA-registered practitioners
- [ ] Verify policy covers all marketed services (including telehealth, cosmetics, audiology)
- [ ] Ensure coverage includes marketing-related claims
- [ ] Practice is liable for ALL marketing content, including third-party agencies
- [ ] Include marketing compliance obligations in agency contracts
- [ ] Disclaimers do not override statutory obligations

## 4.7 Advertising Approvals & Records

- [ ] No formal pre-approval required, but all ads must comply at time of publication
- [ ] For medicines/devices: must align with SAHPRA-approved indications
- [ ] Retain copies of ALL advertisements for minimum 3 years
- [ ] Record date, medium, target audience for each ad
- [ ] Retain evidence supporting any claims made
- [ ] Document approval chain (who drafted, reviewed, authorised)

## 4.8 Complaint Handling

- [ ] HPCSA complaints: respond within 40 working days
- [ ] Possible outcomes: reprimand, fine, formal inquiry, suspension, removal from register
- [ ] ASA complaints: provide written comment, ruling follows
- [ ] Establish internal process for receiving and documenting complaints

## 4.9 Ongoing Compliance

- [ ] **Quarterly**: Review all active marketing materials for compliance
- [ ] **Annually**: POPIA compliance audit (data processing, consent records, third-party DPAs)
- [ ] **Annually**: Update privacy policy
- [ ] **Annually**: Verify HPCSA registration of all marketed practitioners
- [ ] **All staff**: POPIA awareness training at induction and annually
- [ ] **Brief agencies**: HPCSA advertising restrictions and POPIA obligations before campaigns
- [ ] **Report breaches**: POPIA data breaches to Information Regulator "as soon as reasonably possible"
- [ ] **Report**: Adverse events related to marketed medical devices (hearing aids) to SAHPRA
